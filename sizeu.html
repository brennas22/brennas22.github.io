SizeU - Case Study

SizeU

SizeU is an in progress project I am working on as a part of Generate, Northeastern's Student-led Product Development Studio. SizeU is a student-venture that came to Generate for assistance in creating a minimum viable product (MVP) as part of launching their business. SizeU is a company focused on changing sizing in the fashion industry, specifically in online shopping. See SizeU's entire presentation.

The Problem

Design a way to easily take body measurements, BUT do not display any sort of number measurement to the user. Measurements must be transfered to mobile and desktop (software is outside the scope of Generate at this time).

Required measurements:

+ Neck
+ Arm (shoulder to wrist)
+ Inseam
+ Chest
+ Waist

Key considerations:

1. Method - how will the device measure?
2. Margin of error - how will the user know the measurement is accurate if they can't see the number?
3. Shape / size - how will the device feel in the hand? will it even be hand-held?
4. Communication - how will the device send measurements to desktop and mobile devices?

Team

This project is being undertaken by an interdisciplinary team of 6 designers and engineers (top to bottom, left to right): Matt Conniff (computer, project lead), JT Hinchen (mechanical), Daniel Castle (electrical), Chris Tannock (electrical), Kassidy MacPhail (mechanical), and Brenna Sorkin (CS, design).

We decided the best way to proceed with this project was to divide into 2 focuses:

1. Looks-like
+ Kassidy and myself
+ Focus on external pieces and UX
+ Conduct user testing
+ Final product will be a prototype that looks and feels like the final design

2. Works-like
+ JT, Castle and Chris
+ Focus on functionality
+ Figure out how measurements will be recorded and transmitted
+ Final product will be a prototype that functions like the final solution

Research

We began by researching existing electronic measuring tools. We generally found 2 categories:

1. Traditional
+ Bagel: only existing measuring device with traditional string method and wheel mode, not requiring numbers displayed on tape, has 3 modes:
1. String Mode: traditional unravel method with electronically stored measurements
2. Wheel Mode: uses rotations of wheel to measure total length travelled
3. Remote Mode: built in laser pointer, measures length to closest intersecting object
+ all modes transfer measurements to app through bluetooth technology

2. Laser Reflection
+ Fluke 424D
+ Stanley Smart Measure Pro
+ eTape16

We considered these options, but also tried to keep our minds open to thinking outside the box - maybe it doesn't have to resemble a tape measure at all!

Brainstorming

We decided to have a design thinking based 5-minute brainstorm! We came up with some insane ideas, my favorite being robot tailor.

Method: Each team member spent 5 minutes throwing as many ideas as possible (crazy ideas encouraged!) onto post-its and sticking them on the wall. At the end of the 5 minutes we organized the post-its into general themes/categories and then discussed their merits.

Some noteworthy ideas:
+ Computer scanning
+ Standard electronic tape measure with a twist on the housing
+ Stretchy fabric with bands that measure amount expanded
+ 3D scanner

Some truly terrible ideas:
+ Roll around on a mat
+ Stealth tailor stalks you
+ Dog that can sniff out your measurements

Idea selected:
Ultimately, we deiced the best idea to pursue was a standard electronic tape measure, but one that does not display measurements as numbers and is designed specifically to easily take measurements of one's own body.

Defining the User and UX

Once we knew which technological direction we wanted to go in, we needed to pinpoint our user in order to begin designing the external housing. We did this by speaking with Katie, one of SizeU's founders, and creating a user persona of a customer based on our discussion. We also nailed down some details that are central to the user experience that SizeU wants to cultivate for its customers.

Customer Persona:
+ Female
+ Age 18 - 35
+ Young professional
+ Fairly computer/tech savvy
+ Cares about their appearance
+ Wants to easily find clothes that fit

Key Facets of the UX:
+ Does not feel like an evaluation:
+ Evoke the enjoyment that comes from having well-fitting clothes in one’s personal style
+ No visible numbers
+ Housing for tape should not resemble a traditional measuring tape (perhaps no housing at all)
+ Sends a positive message to the user
+ Alternative shapes - the edge of the tape does not need to be straight so long as it doesn’t compromise the measurement
+ Customization is a visible metaphor for breaking the mold of predetermined sizing

Iteration 1

We began sketching by hand and then converted our ideas into solidworks

+ Loosely based on a nunchuck shape
+ Snaps together to form one whole shape when closed - probably using magnets
+ Easy to hold for a variety of measurement positions
+ Considers assembly and functional parts
+ Is durable
+ One half houses electronic components, other is empty
+ String (or tape) connects the two halves

Iteration 2

At this point in the project (and every second week since) we met with a number of industry professionals for advice on our designs

I took a lot of notes at the meeting, but some of the important highlights were:
+ How are we preventing the string from being damaged? - recess the string?
+ Look into anthropomorphic tables for hands when considering sizing of the device
+ What about a hook on the end?
+ How are we taking leg measurements?
+ Consider how the weight of each half will differ
+ What buttons are necessary?
+ What sort of feedback will the user get?

We then began a second iteration of sketching considering the feedback we'd received

Design review notes

Second round of sketches

As a next step, we took a few of our ideas from our sketches and modeled them in clay to get a better feel for the size and shape, paying particular attention to how they sit in the hand. We used retracting ID badges to simulate the functional aspect of the tape measure and placed LEDs and buttons from an arduino kit to test placement.

Idea #1: All electronic components placed in one part, the other part is a combination ring/plate at 90 degrees to each other. This makes it easy to step on for leg measurements and also to hold for arms. Pictured: me looking extremely uncomfortable modeling the use of this product

Idea #2: All electronic components placed in 1 part, the other part is a ring that can be looped around a finger for easy arm measurement or stepped on for inseam. Again featuring me looking extremely uncomfortable

Idea #3: Similar to idea #1 except the plate and loop are along the same plane. I look way less uncomfortable in this one!!

Design Selection

In order to select one of our 3 ideas as a front-runner (though we intend to continue prototyping multiple), we placed our models in the hands of anyone who came into the Sherman Center.

Feedback:

Idea #1:
Honestly, everyone (including Kassidy and me) hated this idea. The thumb piece was extremely awkward to use and did not feel at all ergonomic to the shape of the hand. Users did like how nicely the two halves fit together though.

Idea #2:
This idea was much more popular; People noted that it was very clear how to hold the device to take measurements and they felt comfortable with the placement of the button. However, we received feedback that the two pieces could fit together better.

Idea #3:
Though similar to Idea #1, Idea #3 was generally received as more comfortable to hold, although the large plate still felt uncomfortable with one's thumb looped through the end. It was mentioned that this design would be the easiest/most intuitive to step on for taking a leg measurement.

Selection:

We decided to pursue idea #2 because the distribution of weight felt the most comfortable and it was the most intuitive to use. We then created a storyboard of the user experience from unboxing through recording a complete measurement to begin thinking more about the buttons and indicators that would be needed.

App

On my own time, I decided to do a mock-up of how I envision the app looking.

I considered:
+ Color palette

+ Menus

Sketches for the SizeU app

I used Sketch and Principle to create the clickable walkthrough, which I then recorded as a video and placed inside an iphone mock-up using photoshop and AfterEffects

I created a simple walkthrough that takes the user from login through recording a new measurement and also highlights the menu options. In this app the user really only has 1 work flow, recording a measurement, which is why I decided to make that extremely easy to access. I only included the hamburger menu for far less likely actions (Viewing the about page or logging out of the app.)
