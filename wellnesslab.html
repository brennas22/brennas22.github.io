Wellness Technology Lab - Case Study

Wellness Technology Lab

In Interaction Design, we were tasked with creating an interface for desktop and mobile that allows a user to keep track of some sort of data.

A problem that struck very close to home for me, being a transfer student, was the college search process. While there are many websites that allow students to search by criteria, there isn't a way to save information from various sources all in one place; a student is considering not only the raw stats of the school, but also news, pictures and student testimonials all of which are found in many places on the web.

This video is a walkthrough of the desktop version of Clip.

Features

Clip allows users to filter schools based on a number of criteria. Once they've found a school they're interested in they can create a collection centered around that school. Then, a plug-in (both mobile and desktop) allows them to save external content to the school's collection, which they can then go back to in Clip and organize however they choose.

Recent trends in web technology have shown a large uptick and continued growth in the number of mobile users, especially when it comes to browsing content.

In the mobile version of Clip the user can view their predetermined choices as well as see school matches, make and edit collections and, perhaps most importantly for mobile, add external items. The mobile version of Clip makes it extremely easy for users to save any content they come across directly to the relevant collection, where they can then view it on desktop as well.

User Personas

The very first step in this project was, of course, identifying the user. As an exercise to begin thinking about the user, their needs, and what data they might need to keep track of, I created 3 user personas.

Persona 1: Attorney
Fictional Name: Jane Smith
Major Responsibilities: Works with disabled children and their families on IEPs and negotiations with schools
Goals and Tasks: Keep track of client information, spends time researching policy in past cases, and contacting and conversing with parents, schools, and other attorneys.
Environment: Decently comfortable using the basic features of a computer and cell phone, but not beyond that. Works from home, mainly via Skype and email. Is responsive to emails, but struggles when technical issues arise.
Things they might track: Payments, client information (address, phone number etc.), case status

Persona 2: Art Student
Fictional Name: Kyra Elliot
Major Responsibilities: Studying fine art: interested in painting, drawing, photography, fashion, and printmaking
Goals and Tasks: Research for projects, look for inspiration, post to her blog, shop for supplies, listen to music
Environment: Extremely comfortable with computers and most modern technologies. Is away at school running on slower wifi. Spends large chunks of time away from a computer working on art, but is always on her cell phone
Things they might track: Inspirational posts, viewership on her blog, trending music

Persona 3: High School Senior
Fictional Name: KT Brady
Major Responsibilities: High school student applying to college, works as a lifeguard in the summer
Goals and Tasks: Look at colleges, talk to friends on facebook, count instagram likes
Environment: Extremely comfortable with all modern technologies and is up to date on current trends. Is always connected to her cell phone and responds in under 5 minutes
Things they might track: grades, potential colleges, social media following

Creating these personas I had to consider the wide variety of data that people from all different walks of life are likely to be tracking. I was very drawn to the third persona of a high school student looking for the right college due to my own experience as a transfer student. I wish that I had more organized information available to me during my college search process both times. It occured to me that this issue affects not only high school seniors and transfers, but also potentially students applying to graduate school or Ph.D. programs as well. I did sketches for a search and information organization engine for both school and job searches because, while the criteria are different, the methodology is similar.

Sketches

I began my process of identifying and solving a problem by examining existing products. My initial idea was a job search board that would allow you to store documents, etc.. I was inspired by a combination of the job search engine Monster.com and social media site Pinterest.com. I then began to tweak and expand my idea of storing imporant information on a variety of boards.

User Analysis

Once I settled on a college search and information organization site I began to do a more in-depth analysis of my potential users and their needs. I did this through storyboarding a user's potential experience as well as charting their goals.

Initial Layout

As a next step I created a preliminary layout using Balsamiq to make a clickable walkthrough that I then used to do user testing.

User Testing

Participants: 4 high school students, 2 boys, 2 girls

Tasks:

1. Search for a 4 year school with SAT 2250 that is within 100 miles of 02115 (Boston) and add it to a collection
2. Find collections, view in groups, and move an item from 1 collection to another

Results:


Successes:

1. Users were able to seamlessly log in
2. Users understood to click/drag requirements they wanted to adjust
3. Users liked the preview of additional information "this is nice because if I change my mind I can easily click away"
4. Participants were able to locate group view


Key Issues:

1. Pages were overly crowded and users were slowed down by too much information
2. Users wanted additional descriptions in order to tell collections apart
3. It was unclear which items belonged in which groups within a collection
4. It was unclear how to move an item from one group to another

Visual Design Round 1

My next task was to process user feedback and begin to make a visual design for the website on both desktop and mobile

Icon Design

At this point in the process I began to sketch ideas for a logo. I had already decided on the name Clip for the product so I began my sketches with ideas evoked by the word "clip" (scissors, paperclips etc.). I iterated through a series of different sketches, refining my ideas until I selected the final icon.


First iteration of icons (above) done with pen in my sketchbook and evaluated by my professor and other students in the class. The second iteration (right) improves upon the most widely selected group.

Type Studies

I also did a series of type studies, examining different fonts, font-weights and sizes for signifying varied importance within the product.

Final Designs

Utilizing feedback about the logo, type, visual, and interaction design, I did a major visual overhaul and created this final design and a clickable version using Sketch and InVision (for web) and Adobe XD (for mobile).

Key Takeaways

This project was the first that I completed end to end, from problem identification all the way through a complete design. I learned a lot about interacting with users and the design thinking process as well as some technical skills such as Balsamiq, Adobe XD, InVision and user testing. I was also able to strengthen preexisting skills such as sketching and taking feedback in order to effectively iterate. If I were to change an aspect of my process or final product I would do more extensive user testing after my first round of visual design because I made a significant number of organizational changes.
