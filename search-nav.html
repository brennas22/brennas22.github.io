Search and Navigation System for Toast Back Office

Search and Navigation System for Toast Back Office

Toast did not have a cohesive experience to navigate, nor an overall operating system, meaning that users needed to memorize the layout of the entire site in order to quick find the things they need. This caused a ton of cognitive load, but also made the user base extremely resistant to change.

Goals

Create a consistent experience across the whole system so users can intuit instead of memorizing.

Reduce time to navigate and bounce rate (multiple nav clicks back to back), to help users find what they need faster.

I led design to completely overhaul the existing search and navigation experience in the Toast Web "Back Office" and created a consistent operating system for all of Toast. I created guidelines and worked with literally every team in Toast to place their pages in the navigation, creating a new paradigm for the future of the entire system.

Existing Toast Web homepage

Challenges

Users do not know where to find particular items

The existing high-level categories do not provide users with confidence about what they will find behind a click. They told us they hate being "whisked away" to some other page, where they don't know what they will find.

Existing "left nav," collapsed

Existing "left nav," expanded

A previous team had attempted to reorganize the navigation, and while they did add some key structure, the top level categories remained the same and users still felt they had to guess what was in each section.

An previous experimental update to the navigation (now gone).

Clicks only lead to more clicks

Once a user guesses at the top level category, they don't actually reach a destination; instead, they're most often directed to a "category page," which is a list of more links within that topic. The user then has to parse these links and decide if in fact any of them are what they are looking for.

One of many category pages.

Multiple disjointed experiences exist in tandem

In addition to category pages, there exist "sub-navs" within sections of Toast Web. These are all designed, built, and owned by different teams, with no relationship to each other, and completely different code under the hood. They all follow different structures, which causes users to read the entire list or memorize how to get to things they need.

Examples of different sub-navigations across the system.

Coordination with other teams

Every single team at Toast has some sort of presence in Toast Web, and has a vested stake in making sure their pages are highlighted, which can at times be at odds with the user JTBD, so we needed to balance the varying goals and interests.

What I did

Conducted research + achieved alignment

We did not have a clear and complete understanding of how users were navigating around and thinking about the Toast Back Office. My colleague, Bjorn, conducted a number of conversations, where we learned that a lot of user frustration with the existing navigation came from needing to "learn the system," leaving users feeling like the rug was pulled out from under them any time there was a change.

I shared my findings with my product manager and engineering team to ensure that everyone understood the problems our users were facing at the end of the night.

Some notes from my observations at The Lexington in Cambridge, MA

I mapped the dependencies within the end of day experience. I met with each of the designers on the teams who's work connects to end of day, creating a bi-weekly "end of day-ers unite!" meeting to maintain alignment and share ideas. We discussed opportunities for the close out day experience to connect with time entries (when employees clock in and out), reporting, our mobile app, and more.

Evaluated hypotheses

I began exploring a few different concepts in Figma, centered around the idea of making it clear what tasks remain incomplete, and what steps should be taken to complete them. I prioritized information density and experimented with different tag colors and styling to help managers understand the state of their restaurant at a glance.

An early draft of the end of day experience

I started with a modular approach, thinking of close out day like a dashboard. My hypothesis was that a dashboard-style view would enable users to mentally chunk the information into digestible pieces and allow users to mentally categorize where to start.

I continued my restaurant visits and began showing these early drafts to operators to get their feedback. While users found the additional information and context useful for understanding the state of things, they informed me that they think of close out as a checklist, so a linear layout might be more intuitive.

A draft of a vertical view of close out day

Final designs

New end of day experience

I incorporated this feebdack, opting for a more vertical approach, but keeping the augmented information. I also explored different mechanisms for breaking apart the existing auto-resolve feature. Originally the feature clocked out all employees and closed all paid checks without adding a tip, but the combined functionality was intimidating and many users didn't even know about the feature.

New end of day experience - completed

Results *chefs kiss*

By redesigning the close out day experience we were able to save users time (allowing them to get home faster!) and help managers understand which tasks are most important and how to best resolve them.

A new approach

At the beginning of this project, the brief was to simply to update the style of the screen to use the new POS 3.0 design system. However, I identified a number of paper cuts and greater problems and determined that this was an opportunity to improve the user experience beyond just the look and feel.

This project also exposed the interdependencies between different experiences within the Toast ecosystem. While these features are owned by different teams, from the user perspective they are deeply interconnected. This project kicked off closer collaboration between teams whose work impacts the end of day experience, laying the groundwork for a more cohesive experience.
