unCommon Foods - Case Study

unCommon Foods

In my Experience Design class we were tasked with creating an intervention in the parks in Boston. I designed a new food experience that would emphasize local sustainable food.

I developed a business plan for funding the experience, as well as the structures that would be placed in the commons, and an app to facilitate navigation of the new features.

this video is a short synopsis of the project

The solution that I created has 3 main facets:

1. A business plan that sustains local up and coming chefs

+ Investors provide money to maintain the kiosks in the park as well as funding to the chefs.
+ The chefs in the program are not already established in the Boston food scene, but can apply and be selected for the program.
+ The funding from the investors allows the chefs to put together a special menu to be served in the common for a limited time.
+ Customers order the food via app (below) and provide feedback.
+ This feedback helps the chefs improve, but also helps the investors know which chefs have the best potential to be successful in a full scale restaurant or food truck.
+ The quick-changing cycle of chefs in the kiosks allows a vast array of new chefs to showcase their talents and potentially find an investor, which helps the Boston food scene grow, pushing out the fast food in the area and bringing a more local and authentic feel to the Boston Common, which is often known as the quintessential Boston sight to see for tourists.

2. An easy to use app
The app allows customers to look at information about chefs, browse the menu, place and order, know when that order is ready, and submit reviews.

3. The physical structures
I designed 3 structures, each with a unique feel and personality, to be placed in the Boston Common. They are designed to fit into the vibe of the park and highlight Boston's unique vibrance.

App

I did an initial mock up of the app that would be used for ordering food in the park in November 2016 using Adobe Experience Design (images can be found at the bottom of the page). I recently (February 2017) decided that my work could be improved. Using Sketch and learning Principle for the first time, I redesigned the app and added animations and special transitions.

Enthnographic Study


This project began with an ethnographic study in and around Boston's public park system, The Emerald Necklace. During one of our first classes we traveled to the Back Bay Fens, part of the Emerald necklace located near Northeastern, and I created an experience map of my personal interpretation of the outing (right).

I chose to focus my study on the Boston Common because of its tendency to attract many tourists. During my first visit I only observed patrons in a natural habitat and took pictures (below) so as not to corrupt my findings. I observed a number of fast food stands and restaurants surrounding the park and noted that people either purchased that food or brought their own and enjoyed a meal picnic style.

These observations led me to formulate a number of open ended questions to ask patrons during my next visit. I asked about their time spent in the common (frequency, activity, etc.) and then asked them to tell me their feelings about the food scene in the area. Ultimately I decided to pursue the topic of healthy, local food in the commons as the core concept for my semester long project.


Below is the poster created to summarize my findings from my ethnographic research. It notes the variety of patrons in the Boston Common as well as their needs and desires, along with a list of notable problems that I either observed or learned about through interviews.

Research

Once I decided to build structures in the park I began researching convertible structures or ones that easily blend into the scenery. I discovered a number of shipping containers that had been converted into restaurants, some that are able to refold into the container and some that are more permanent. (images found via google)

I also found a variety of structures that seamlessly integrate into the landscape, mostly by bringing grass over the top and using wood to bring a natural feel. These structures however were a lot more permanent and not collpasable in any way. (images found via google)

Sketches

I then began sketching 3 different structures to be placed in the Boston Common as well as loosely defining the UX. I designed each of them to have a different vibe and thereby would be home to different types of food, expanding the variety. I was inspired by a number of different sources including my research, some of the architecture near MIT, and sculptural elements from the Back Bay Fens.

I also did some preliminary sketches for the walkthrough of the app, considering both interaction and visual design as well as high tech elements such as augmented reality

Final Presentation

The final presentation for this class was done at the Emerald Necklace Conservancy in the Back Bay Fens, where I was able to recieve feedback from my professor and peers, as well as staff at the Emerald Necklace. The final deliverables consisted of the video (top), poster (top), and slides (below) all with my commentary.

Key Takeaways

I learned a lot from this project about the process of designing an experience. However, the thing that I believe will stick with me most and inform my ability as a designer is something my professor said: "You cannot design an experience. People will do whatever they want and their experience will be based on a number of things outside your control. What you can do instead is design an opportunity."

His words really affected me because I started to back up and look at the problem in terms of the bigger picture. I tried to create an "experience" that would highlight local chefs in Boston, but I also tried to allow for flexibility within these programmed spaces so that people could make the most out of them in whatever way they chose.

I also learned the important skills of ethnographic research and user interviews.
